## 1 The Three Cs: Competence, Commitment, Compatibility

Competence: Can they get the job done well?

Commitment: Are you excited to be here?

Compatibility:  Do you get along with the team?

Your challenge is to have your manager, team and clients answer "Yes" to all three of these questions, otherwise your team will not trust you.

- To be competent: means you can get the job done fully, accurately, and promptly without being micromanaged.
- To be committed means: you are fully present and eager to help your team achieve its goals
- To be compatible: To make others be comfortable and eager to be around you
- Long form

    ### Competence

    You do not want to clueless, nor do you want to be overbearing, you want to be right in the middle of the two: competence.

    Example of clueless: Student got an internship during school semester, got busy with exam and stopped working on job, didnt answer emails, or answer phone. She looked clueless to her manager → results in her getting fired

    Example of overbearing: A new teacher starts to brag about a new lesson planning method, older teachers never want to interact with this person now

    ### Commitment

    You don't want to be apathetic, nor do you want to be threatening

    Example of apathetic: Camp consoler did smile when he was doing his work, so everyone thought he was doing a shitty job

    Example of threatening: Intern work on teamates work without asking, and corrected his manager in front of the higherups → did not get return offer.

    ### Compatibility

    You dont want to be pasive (inautethic), but you also do not want to be a poser (a try hard)

    Inauthentic: A person who worked at a movie teather never made small talk with her manager, results in her getting fired

    Poser: American MBA student is working in Asia. Presentation is over and tradition is senior ask questions first, but American MBA (does not know custom), asks question since it seemed like no one else had question

# Secrets to Getting Started

## 2 Think "Let's give this a shot!"

- Where you start does not have to dictate where you end up
- If you want more than the bare minimum, you will need to do more than the bare minimum
- If the worst thing that can happen is the other person says "no", is risk of failure is not as bad as you think
- Long form

    Sandy was working at an Oil and Gas company right out of college. She was in a program in a 3 year shift where each year you had a different role. She did not enjoy her first rotation, and really wanted to join a different team, however that was not part of the "offical" teams you can join.

    Sandy ended up emailing the SVP of the team, and didnt get a response, however, she ended up messaging her on slack, and gets told that their is no opportunity to in SVP's team atm, however, the SVP invites her to a book club. SVP ends email to female execs and Sandy about book club, asking how they should structure the club. No one else responds to the email, so Sandy ends up coming up with a plan for the book club, and the book club is offically formed. After some time the SVP tells sandy to add a note to the HR form regarding placement that wants to join that team, and ends on that team

    Lessons:

    - where you start does not dictate where you end up
        - Sandy started in a shitty position, but she put in the work and ended up somewhere she enjoyed
    - If you want more than the bare minimum, you need to put in the work:
        - If sandy didnt attempt to make a connection with the SVP, nothing would have happened, and you likely would have ended up in a shitty spot
    - If the worst answer is No just ask
        - What was the worst that happened to sandy after asking: they said no, but she still had the opportunity to connect with others

## 3 Show up Like a high performer

- Before first day of work, skim the organization's website - "about us", "what we do", "news", "team", and lookup the company on social media
    - This helps you understand what the company does, and what it has been doing lately and who the most important people are
    - Look at page ~35 to see more important things to do
- Make sure you ae clear on what you should wear, where you should go, when you should show up and what you should bring for your first day
- When you are new, focus on asking questions, specifically questions you wan't answer yourself. Use the structure "Here is my question, and here is why I am asking this question" or "Here is what I know, and here is what I do not know"

## 4 Be Proactive

> A key difference between school and work: school is about *keeping up*; work is about *stepping up*

- If no one introduces themselves, you introduce yourself
- If no one shares information with you, you ask for the information
- If no ones finds you work, you find work.

Don't be afraid of asking questions

# Secrets to Shaping Other's Perceptions

## 5 Know How to Tell your Story

- The clear you are on what you want from your new role, the better your chances of designing a fulfilling experience for yourself
- The better you can explain how your past, present and future selves fit with this job, the more competent and committed you will come across
- Write down what is your internal narrative: What do you want out of this experience?
- Be ready to share your external narrative: How does who you are and what you've done make you competent, committed and compatible for your. new role?
- Figure out what your audience is intrested in hearing, then give them what they want
- Frame your story in terms of past present and future, just like a Hero's journey
- When speaking try to be confident, but not arrogant, but not robotic and upbeat, but not immature

## 6 Manage Your Appearance

- Consider what impressions your appearance will give your managers, coworkers, clients and partners
- Make deliberate choices about your clothes, accessories and grooming
- Recognize patterns in other's appearances, choices and using them as a starting point when deciding your own appearance.
- Figure out when you should reject the rules, embrace the rules, or bend the rules
- Decide for yourself what you consider sacred, negotiable and indifferent when it comes to your appearance
